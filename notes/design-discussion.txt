⏺ Good question to start with, because the "a wire has exactly two endpoints"
  premise is doing more work in this design than it first appears, and it's a
  genuinely independent decision from the requirements.

  First, let me be precise about what the current design actually commits to,
  since it's easy to conflate two separate things:

  1. The electrical model is already net-based and id-based. FR-034b/FR-059a
  require that nets be derivable without pixel geometry, and §6.6 satisfies that
  with union-find over pin:* and wire:* ids. So connectivity correctness does
  not depend on the two-endpoint rule.
  2. The two-endpoint rule is a separate, geometric/record-shape choice. A
  "wire" here is a polyline (it has bends) but topologically an edge with
  exactly two endpoints a and b. Fan-out is pushed onto two mechanisms (A2):
  multiple wires sharing a pin endpoint, and a junction endpoint where one
  wire's endpoint references another wire's id.

  So your real question is: was edge-with-two-endpoints +
  junction-references-target-wire the best way to represent topology? There are
  at least three alternatives, and I think one of them is cleaner.
  
  Alternative A — Node-centric graph (explicit vertices)

  Make vertices first-class objects with ids. A vertex is one of: pin-anchor
  (U3.Y0), junction, or free/dangling point. A wire segment is just an edge
  {vertexA, vertexB}. Bends are degree-2 vertices (or stay as interior polyline
  points); junctions and fan-out are simply vertices of degree ≥ 3.

  - Net derivation becomes the natural connected-components of the graph —
  arguably even simpler than the current union-find, because a shared vertex id
  directly encodes a connection. No "endpoint kind" switch.
  - The G2 problem disappears. In the current design (§3.3) deleting a wire
  orphans every other wire whose junction endpoint referenced it, forcing the
  "retarget junction → free" cleanup. That's a symptom of asymmetry: a junction
  is modeled as "wire X's endpoint lands on wire Y," giving Y a parent-ish role
  over X. With shared vertices there's no target wire — deleting an edge just
  removes an edge; the shared vertex survives if anything else touches it, or
  gets swept if not.
  - It also fixes a latent visual bug. Today a junction stores a render-only
  coordinate "on" wire Y. If Y is later reshaped (a bend dragged), that
  coordinate can drift off Y's actual geometry while staying electrically
  connected. A shared vertex can't float off, because both segments are defined
  by it.
  - Cost: more objects and referential-integrity bookkeeping (no dangling vertex
  refs), and "branch from a wire" becomes "split the target edge at a new
  vertex, reuse it" rather than "store a coordinate + target id."

  This is the textbook schematic-as-planar-graph representation, and I think
  it's strictly better on the connectivity axis. The current design is
  essentially an edge-centric encoding of the same graph that keeps vertices
  implicit — which is why it needs the junction-by-wire-id indirection and the
  G2 special case.

  Alternative B — Net-centric (nets authoritative, wires cosmetic)

  Make nets the stored primary object: pins carry a netId, and wires become
  purely graphical polylines tagged with a netId. This is how real EDA tools
  work (it's what enables net labels — two wires on the same net that don't
  visually touch).

  - Pro: FR-059a is trivially satisfied because connectivity is stored, not
  derived; downstream tools read nets directly.
  - Con: you move the union-find work from save-time to edit-time. Connecting
  two wires must merge two nets (and pick a surviving id); deleting a wire may
  split a net (recompute). That's more complex interaction code than the
  requirements ask for, and it permits geometry/electrical disagreement (a
  feature for net-labels, a footgun otherwise). For this phase it's probably
  over-built.

  Alternative C — Hyperedge wire (one wire = one whole net-fragment)

  Let a single wire be a tree/polyline with N endpoints, so one wire object is a
  connected sub-net. Fan-out is intrinsic; no junction-to-other-wire concept at
  all.
  
  - Pro: no junction indirection; drawing a branch just extends the same wire.
  - Con: the record stops being a simple two-point edge,
  hit-testing/bend-editing on an N-ary tree is fiddlier, and you still need
  pin-sharing to union two separate wires — so you don't fully escape union-find
  anyway.
  
  My read

  The current choice is defensible and minimal, and it does satisfy the
  requirements. But the one wart it carries — the junction asymmetry that forces
  G2 and the "render-only coordinate that can drift" — is exactly the thing
  Alternative A removes for a modest cost. If I were to change one thing, it
  would be to keep two-endpoint segments but make a junction reference a shared
  vertex id instead of a target wire id. That's the smallest move that converts
  the design from edge-centric-with-implicit-vertices to a clean graph, and it
  deletes a special case rather than adding machinery.

  A couple of things worth your view, since this is the discussion:

  - Do you anticipate ever wanting net labels / virtual connections (signals
  connected by name, not by drawn wire)? If yes, that pulls toward Alternative B
  and is worth deciding now, because it's expensive to retrofit. If no, A is
  the sweet spot.
  - How attached are you to the junction's "point along a wire" semantics — is a
  junction conceptually on a particular wire, or just a shared point where
  things meet? The current doc implies the former; A assumes the latter.

⏺ Yes — supportive, and let me be precise about why, because the connector tool
  sharpens the argument in a way that's worth getting exactly right.

  The connector is, in essence, a named electrical node with identity. A wire
  terminates at it, and its electrical meaning is "this point is the same net as
  the matching A6 on another sheet." That's three properties: it's a vertex, it
  has a stable identity/label, and it participates in net derivation by
  identity rather than by physical adjacency. Alternative A's whole premise is
  "electrical nodes are first-class objects with ids." So a connector drops in
  as just another vertex type, and cross-sheet linkage becomes one more union
  rule in buildNets: union all connector vertices that share a label. That sits
  right alongside the rules you already have (union pin↔segment, union
  segment↔segment-at-shared-vertex).

  Now the honest qualifier, so you're not over-weighting this one data point:

  - A connector by itself doesn't stress the two-endpoint rule. It's a degree-1 
  terminator — a wire ends there. The edge-centric model could absorb it too,
  just by adding kind: "connector" to the Endpoint union. So the connector alone
  isn't a knockout argument for A; the knockout argument remains the junction 
  asymmetry (G2) and the drift-prone render coordinate.
  - What the connector adds is a trend, and the trend is the real signal. You've
  now named a sequence of features that are all "named electrical nodes with
  identity": junctions today, off-sheet connectors next, and you floated
  edge-connector components after that. In Alternative A each of these is the 
  same concept — a vertex with a type and maybe a label — so each lands as a
  small, uniform addition: one vertex type + one union rule. In the edge-centric
  model each one is a new special case accreting onto the Endpoint union, and
  it lands next to the junction kind that's already the source of G2. So the
  connector roadmap is evidence that "node with identity" wants to be a
  first-class concept, which is exactly what A makes it. That's the supportive
  part.

  Two things worth flagging before you commit, though, because the connector
  pulls in scope the current design doesn't have:

  1. It's a controlled re-introduction of connection-by-identity. You said no
  invisible net labels, and that still holds — but the connector is
  connection-by-name; it's just visible and explicit (a drawn symbol with A6 in
  it). That distinction is exactly the right line to draw, and it's worth
  recording so a future reader doesn't think the two positions contradict. The
  netlist will eventually union two vertices that are nowhere near each other
  geometrically, on the basis of a shared label. A handles that cleanly; just
  know you're signing up for it.
  2. Connectors imply a sheet concept that the design doesn't have yet. Today

  change, so the two don't get conflated.

I pushed it. Let's discuss the bus-as-N-nets question now

⏺ Good — this is the one place the current §6.6/§7.2 quietly cheats, so let's
  pin it down.

  The core problem

  A bus is not one net. An 8-bit DATA bus is 8 independent nets that happen to
  be drawn as one thick line for tidiness. But the current buildNets (even after
  the vertex rewrite) unions all of a bus's path vertices into a single
  connected component — so it would emit one net per bus instead of width nets.
  That's silently wrong, and since §7.2 advertises nets as a derived convenience
  for the downstream simulator/transpiler (A4), shipping a wrong nets array is
  worse than shipping none.

  What's actually already right

  Here's the good news: the data model already stores everything needed for
  per-bit nets. Bus.width gives the bit count, and each groupConnection carries
  bitMap — an ordered list of component pins. The ordering is the bit binding:
  bus bit i ↔ bitMap[i]. That's exactly FR-042 ("auto-connect each bit to the
  corresponding pin in declared order"). So the fix is almost entirely in the
  algorithm, not the schema.

  The fix: two-level netlist (graphical group, then bit expansion)

  Keep the vertex union-find for graphical connectivity, then layer bit-indexed
  nets on top:

  # Step A — graphical groups (unchanged): which edges are physically joined
  guf = UnionFind(vertex ids)
  for each edge E in wires ∪ buses: union all node-vertices in E.path
  # → group(E): buses joined at a shared junction vertex land in one group

  # Step B — width per group + homogeneity check
  for each graphical group G:
      widths = { width(E) : edges E in G }      # wire = 1, bus = bus.width
      if |widths| > 1: FLAG "mixed-width connection"   # see decision Q3
      w(G) = the single width

  # Step C — per-bit nets, keyed (group, bitIndex)
  net[(G,i)] = { pins:set, members:set }
  for each edge E with group G:
      for i in 0..w(G)-1: net[(G,i)].members += E.id
      if E is a 1-bit wire:
          for each pin-vertex pv on E: net[(G,0)].pins += "U_.pin"
      if E is a bus:
          for each groupConnection gc on E:
              for i, pinName in enumerate(gc.bitMap):
                  net[(G,i)].pins += gc.instance + "." + pinName
  emit every net[(G,i)] whose pins is non-empty

  This falls out cleanly:
  - Plain wire → w=1, one net per group: identical to today's behavior.
  - Bus → w nets per group, pins joined by bit index.
  - Bus-to-bus junction → both buses in one group, so bit i of each lands in
  net[(G,i)]: bit-aligned join, automatically. The vertex graph you already
  built does the alignment work.

  The one new invariant is Step B's homogeneity check: every edge in a graphical
  group must have the same width. That's what catches "an 8-bit bus joined to a
  4-bit bus" or "a plain wire spliced into a bus."

  Three things this surfaces that need your call

  Q1 — Do we compute bus nets in this phase at all? Either fix buildNets to
  expand bits as above (correct, ~30 lines), or explicitly scope it so nets
  covers only wires this phase and document that bus nets are the downstream
  tool's job. I strongly prefer fixing it now — the model already holds the
  data, the algorithm is small, and a half-correct nets array is a trap for the
  next phase.

  Q2 — Single-bit bus tap (a "bus rip"). This is the real gap. Today you can
  connect a whole pin group to a bus (FR-041/042) or fall back to one nearest
  pin (FR-043), but there's no way to pull one ordinary wire off bit 3 of a bus
  and run it to a single pin. Real schematics do this constantly. The
  requirements don't mention it, so it's arguably out of scope — but a bus you
  can't tap individual bits from is half-useful, and supporting it later means
  giving a junction vertex an optional bit index (so a 1-bit wire can attach to
  "bus b3, bit 3"), which also relaxes the Step B homogeneity rule. Worth
  deciding now whether to design the hook or explicitly defer it.

  Q3 — Width-mismatch handling. When Step B finds a mixed-width group, what's
  the behavior — refuse the connection at interaction time (UI prevents it), or
  allow it and emit a flagged/partial net? I lean "prevent at interaction time,
  and have buildNets treat any mismatch it still sees as a skipped net with a
  logged warning" (defense in depth).

  One smaller schema question rides along: should each emitted Net carry
  provenance — e.g., {bus:"b3", bit:3, name:"DATA3"} — so the downstream
  transpiler can label signals, or stay a bare {pins, members}? Cheap to add,
  genuinely useful downstream, but not required by anything yet.

  What this adds: named bits (provenance) and breakout, unified

  Your flags bus carries named signals (C/V/N/Z), and you want breakout. Those
  two pull in the same small set of additions, and they make me revise the net
  algorithm from "expand bus into bits" to the fully-general bit-lane form:

  Treat every conductor as a lane:
  - a plain wire = 1 lane;
  - a bus of width w = lanes (busId, 0…w-1).

  Then ordinary union-find over lanes:
  for each wire pin-end:            lane(wire) gets that pin
  for each two wires sharing a vertex:        union their lanes
  for each bus groupConnection (bit i ↔ pin): lane(bus,i) gets that pin
  for each bus↔bus full junction (equal width): for i: union lane(A,i),
  lane(B,i)
  for each WIRE TAP on bus bit b:             union lane(wire), lane(bus,b)   #
  breakout
  net = each connected lane-group with ≥1 pin
  This subsumes everything: plain wires, whole-bus snaps, bus-to-bus joins, and
  single-bit breakout — all as lane unions. The earlier "homogeneity check"
  stops being a special case: a wire tapping a bus is fine because it joins one
  lane, not the whole width; only a full bus↔bus junction requires equal width
  (enforced at interaction time per your Q3).

  Model additions to support it (small):
  - Vertex: a junction vertex gains an optional bit field. No bit → full join
  (bus-to-bus, all lanes). bit set → single-bit tap (the breakout hook from Q2).
  One field, and it cleanly distinguishes "splice two buses" from "rip one
  signal."
  - Bus: optional bitNames: string[] (length = width). On the first group snap,
  if the bus has no names yet, it adopts the group's member pin names (so your
  flags bus becomes C/V/N/Z and keeps them at the far end and at any breakout).
  Connectivity is always by index; names are advisory/provenance.
  - Net: gains provenance — { pins, members, lanes:[{bus, bit, name?}], name? }
  — so the downstream transpiler can label a net "N" or "DATA3" instead of
  reverse-engineering it from pins.

  Two things to decide, one to note

  Why it matters concretely (not just purism)

  1. Traceability breaks. design.md §10 maps design→FR-IDs, and §11 verifies
  "each requirement." A feature with no requirement ID is an orphan —
  untraceable and unverifiable against intent.
  2. Authority inverts. design.md's own preamble declares requirements.md "the

  3. Scope/MVP vetting is bypassed. Breakout, the disambiguation dialog, and
  named bits are exactly the kind of thing a stakeholder should get to
  prioritize or defer as requirements, before they're baked into a data model.
  4. Intent is lost to future readers. Requirements answer "why must it behave
  this way"; design answers "how." Nobody should have to reverse-engineer "the
  user needs to rip single bits off buses" from a Vertex.bit field.
